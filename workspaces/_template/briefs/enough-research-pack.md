## 1. The idea, in one paragraph (context)

**Enough** answers a question existing retirement tools ignore: not how much you have saved, but **how much you can safely spend each month** without outliving your money — while still funding the lifestyle you want. It treats CPF LIFE as a guaranteed income **floor** and optimises the safe drawdown of everything else (cash, SRS, investments) to fund the **lifestyle gap**, at a stated confidence level, updating with markets. Its wedge is **spending confidence** — a neutral, trustworthy "permission to spend" that CPF, banks, and advisers structurally cannot give. Business model: freemium, B2B2C-led distribution (banks, insurers, employer-wellness platforms) on a flat-fee, no-commission, neutral basis. Phased MAS-compliant regulatory path (educational tool → Financial Adviser licence).

**The biggest unproven assumption** — and therefore research priority #1 — is that retirees *underspend out of fear* and would act on a credible "permission to spend." If that behavioural premise doesn't hold for Singaporeans, the wedge weakens and the model needs reframing.

## 2. Method notes

### Surveys vs interviews (plain language)
- **Survey** = a fixed list of questions, same for everyone, mostly tick-boxes, sent to many people (100–300). Tells you **how many / how much**. Good for *measuring* things you already know to ask about.
- **Interview** = a real 1-to-1 conversation (~30 min, 8–15 people), open-ended, following the person's train of thought. Tells you **why / how people actually think** — including things you'd never have put on a survey.
- **Order matters:** run a few **interviews first** to discover the real problem and catch bad questions, *then* (optionally) run a survey to measure it at scale. The interviews tell you what to measure; the survey measures it.

### Leading questions (and why they're dangerous)
A **leading question** hints at the answer you want, so people agree more than they truly do — giving **false confidence**.
- ❌ Leading: "Would you love an app that finally tells you how much you can safely spend?"
- ✅ Neutral: "How do you currently decide how much to spend each month?"

Your **sample** (the people who answer) is a limited, one-shot resource — hard to recruit, can't be re-asked fresh. So **fix leading/confusing questions before you spend your sample on them.** Interviews are how you catch them: you watch a real person hesitate or misread, and fix the wording first.

### Three bias traps removed in the instruments below
1. **Selling, not asking** — no "would you value…/wouldn't it be helpful…" framing.
2. **Baked-in assumptions** — don't assume people underspend, or that a tool would tell them to spend *more* (numbers "could be higher or lower").
3. **Acquiescence (yes-saying)** — older respondents tend to agree by default. Fix: ask behaviour before attitude; scatter agree/disagree items; include a reverse-worded check.

## 3. Strategy-annotated survey — retirees & near-retirees (full)

_Each section states the hypothesis it tests and the risk if wrong. SC=single choice, MC=multiple choice, L=Likert 1–5, Open=free text, Num=numeric. Branch: route already-retired vs about-to-retire after screening._

### Section A — Screening & segmentation
*Hypothesis: we can reach and segment our real target. Risk: surveying the wrong people and over-reading results.*
- A1 (SC) Singapore Citizen or PR? *(Citizen / PR / Neither → screen out)*
- A2 (SC) Which best describes you? *(Fully retired / Semi-retired / Retiring within 5 years / Retiring later → screen out or hold separate)*
- A3 (SC) Age: 50–54 / 55–59 / 60–64 / 65–69 / 70–74 / 75+
- A4 (SC) Do you have CPF savings incl. CPF LIFE? *(Yes / No / Not sure)*
- A5 (SC) Who manages day-to-day money decisions? *(Me alone / Me with spouse / Spouse mainly / An adult child / A financial adviser)*
- A6 (SC) Housing: Own HDB (paid) / Own HDB (paying) / Own private (paid) / Own private (paying) / Renting / Other
- A7 (SC) Household assets **outside CPF & property**: <$50k / $50k–150k / $150k–300k / $300k–500k / $500k–1m / >$1m / Prefer not to say
> A7 matters: Enough's value is biggest for those with meaningful non-CPF wealth (the "gap" to optimise).

### Section B — The core problem (most important)
*Hypothesis: a real, felt, unsolved "how much can I safely spend?" question exists. Risk: solution looking for a problem.*
- B1 (L) "I know roughly how much I can safely spend each month."
- B2 (L) "I worry I might run out of money during my lifetime."
- B3 (SC) How do you mainly decide how much to spend? *(A clear plan / A rough rule of thumb / My CPF payout / My adviser guides me / I watch my bank balance / No real method)*
- B4 (SC) Ever worked out a safe monthly amount for your whole retirement? *(Yes—tool/adviser / Yes—myself / No but I'd like to / No, never)*
- B5 (Open) In your own words: single biggest money worry in retirement?
- B6 (Rank) Rank 1–5: outliving savings · healthcare/LTC · inflation · being a burden on family · not enjoying retirement enough.
> B5 unprompted + B6 forced-rank reality-check the claim that *spending confidence* is the wedge.

### Section C — Underspending paradox (the wedge)
*Hypothesis: well-funded retirees underspend out of fear and would spend more given credible permission. Risk: the headline behavioural insight may not hold.*
- C1 (SC) Closest to you? *(Spend less than I can afford to be safe / About what I can afford / More than is comfortable / Not sure what I can afford)*
- C2 (SC) If you learned your finances were healthier than you thought, what would you most likely do? *(Spend a bit more / Keep the same / Save it / Give to family / Double-check first)*
- C3 (SC) Since retiring/planning: spent less than planned out of caution / about as planned / more than planned / not sure.
- C4 (SC) Suppose a tool gave you a safe monthly amount — could be higher or lower than now. How would you treat it? *(Adjust to match / Take as a guide, decide myself / Want a person to confirm / Wouldn't trust an app's number)*
- C5 (Open) What would make you trust such a number enough to act on it?
> C4 is decisive: heavy "hold back" / "only if a person said so" weakens the wedge and elevates adviser-assisted/hybrid models.

### Section D — CPF LIFE & "rest of your wealth"
*Hypothesis: people see CPF LIFE as only part of the answer and don't know how to draw down the rest. Risk: if CPF LIFE feels "enough," the floor-and-gap pitch loses force.*
- D1 (L) "I understand how much CPF LIFE will pay me each month."
- D2 (L) "CPF LIFE alone will cover the lifestyle I want."
- D3 (SC) Confidence drawing down savings outside CPF LIFE? (Very → Not at all, 5-pt)
- D4 (SC) Faced CPF top-up / payout-option choices? *(Done it / Plan to / Didn't know I could / Don't understand them)*
- D5 (SC+open) How clear are you on those CPF choices? (clarity 5-pt) + Have you sought help? *(Adviser / CPF / On my own / No)*

### Section E — Healthcare, longevity, inflation (credibility needs)
*Hypothesis: top-of-mind; people want plans stress-tested. Risk: may crowd out the spending-confidence message.*
- E1 (L) "A major health or long-term-care bill could seriously affect my finances."
- E2 (SC) Set aside a specific amount for healthcare/LTC? *(Yes clear amount / Roughly / No / Rely on MediShield/CareShield/insurance)*
- E3 (L) "I worry inflation will make my savings buy much less in 10–20 years."
- E4 (SC) When you think how long your money must last, what age do you plan to? *(Haven't picked / late 70s / 80s / early 90s / 95+ / Whatever CPF/adviser assumes)*

### Section F — Current tools, advice & trust (competitive landscape)
*Hypothesis: existing tools don't answer the spend question; neutrality differentiates. Risk: people may be happy with bank/adviser, or distrust ALL apps regardless of neutrality.*
- F1 (MC) What do you use today to plan retirement spending? *(CPF calculators / Bank or insurer tools / Human adviser / Spreadsheets / Family / Nothing)*
- F2 (MC) When you receive financial advice, what makes you trust it most? **(pick up to 2)** *(Qualifications/track record / Personal relationship / Big well-known institution / No commission on what they recommend / Government/regulator backing / Friends & family)*
- F3 (SC) Who would you trust most to tell you how much you can safely spend? *(CPF Board / My bank / An independent app / A human adviser / A govt agency / Family / No one)*
- F4 (SC+open) If the app were provided by your bank/insurer, how would that affect your trust? *(More / No difference / Less / Depends)* — Why?
- F5 (Open) What would worry you most about using an app for retirement money?
> F3+F4 test the B2B2C-via-banks plan against the "neutrality is the moat" claim.

### Section G — Concept reaction (show idea HERE, not before)
*Hypothesis: concept understood and wanted. Risk: concept tests over-flatter; anchor with behaviour + money.*
Show 2–3 sentence description + one "Your Monthly Paycheck" mock screen.
- G1 (L) "I understand what this app does."
- G2 (L+open) How well does this match a problem you have? (5-pt) + What problem, if any, does it solve for you?
- G3 (L) "I would try this."
- G4 (SC) The ONE thing that would most make you trust the number? *(Showing assumptions / Plain-English explanation / Backed by real market history / Endorsed by CPF or MAS / Recommended by my adviser / A person I can call)*
- G5 (Open) After reading this — what's confusing or missing?

### Section H — Feature prioritisation
*Hypothesis: six features map to felt needs; we can cut to four. Risk: building all six when two carry the value.*
- H1 (rank/MaxDiff, pick top 3): Safe monthly-spend number · "Will it last to 95?" reading · "What if a big health bill hits?" test · Today's-vs-future-dollars view · CPF top-up & payout guidance · Trips/gifts/inheritance planning · Plain-English "why" · Shared view for family.
> Directly answers the open item: keep 6 features or collapse to 4.

### Section I — Willingness to pay & business model
*Hypothesis: viable freemium + B2B2C. Risk: retirees are price-sensitive and may expect it free (like CPF tools).*
- I1 (SC) Would you pay? *(Yes monthly / Yes one-time / Only if free / Only if bank/employer gives it free)*
- I2 (Van Westendorp, 4 Num) At what monthly price is it: too cheap to trust / a good deal / getting expensive / too expensive?
- I3 (SC) If your bank/insurer offered it free, how likely to use it? (Definitely → Definitely not, 5-pt)
- I4 (Open) What, if anything, would make you hesitate with a bank-provided version?
- I5 (SC) Who *should* pay for this? *(Me / My bank / Government / Employer / My adviser)*
- I6 (SC) Pay more for a version your adviser uses *with* you? *(Yes / No / I don't use an adviser)*
> I3 is the crux of the B2B2C-led plan: does the bank channel help adoption or trigger the distrust the neutrality wedge depends on?

### Section J — Digital readiness & data trust (feasibility gate)
*Hypothesis: this cohort will use an app and share financial data. Risk: make-or-break feasibility question.*
- J1 (SC) Comfort using phone apps for money (e.g. banking app)? (Very → Not at all, 5-pt)
- J2 (SC) Connect your CPF/bank/investment info to an app for a personalised plan? *(Yes, automatically / Yes, type it myself / No, too risky / Only with help from family or a person)*
- J3 (L) "I'd need someone to help me set this up the first time."
- J4 (SC) If not you, who would actually use it? *(Me / My spouse / My adult child on my behalf / No one)*
- J5 (L) "I'm worried about my financial data being leaked or misused."
> J2+J4 are existential: if most won't connect data and expect an adult child to operate it, this is really a **family tool** — design/marketing change.

### Section K — Family & legacy
- K1 (L) "I want to leave money or property to my family."
- K2 (L) "Wanting to leave an inheritance limits how much I let myself spend."
- K3 (SC) Want spouse/children to see your plan in the app? *(Yes all / Yes some / No)*
- K4 (SC) Do your adult children help with your financial decisions? *(A lot / Sometimes / No)*

### Section L — Closing
- L1 (SC) Open to a 20-min follow-up interview? *(Yes + contact / No)*
- L2 (Open) Anything else about retirement money this kind of tool should address?

## 4. Leading-question audit & neutral rewordings

| # | Original (leading) | Why it nudges | Neutral reword |
|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | "I could probably afford to spend more than I do, but I hold back to be safe." | Assumes underspending; double-barrelled. | "Which is closest to you? Spend less than I can afford / about what I can afford / more than is comfortable / not sure." |
| C2 | "I'd feel comfortable spending more if a trustworthy source confirmed it was safe." | Describes our exact wedge → polite yes. | "If you learned your finances were healthier than you thought, what would you most likely do?" |
| C4 | "If a tool told you you can safely spend **$X more**…" | "More" pre-loads underspending. | "A safe monthly amount — **could be higher or lower** than now. How would you treat it?" |
| D5 | "I would value help deciding how much to top up CPF…" | "Would value help" sells the feature. | "How clear are you on your CPF top-up/payout choices?" + "Have you sought help?" |
| E4 | "I'd want any plan to show what happens if I live to 90 or 95." | "I'd want…" endorses a feature. | "When you think how long your money must last, what age do you plan to?" |
| F2 | "I trust advice more when the company earns NO commission." | States the 'right' answer inside the question (and this is the core moat — must not flatter). | "What makes you trust advice most? (pick 2)" — let no-commission compete with qualifications, relationship, brand, regulator. |
| F4 | "I'd be cautious about a bank-run app **because they push products**." | Supplies the reason to distrust. | "If provided by your bank/insurer, how would that affect your trust? More/no difference/less — why?" |
| I3 | reject option "No — I'd worry about **hidden selling**." | Reject option editorialises. | 5-pt likelihood scale + neutral open: "What would make you hesitate?" |
| G2 | "This solves a problem I **actually** have." | "Actually" invites agreement. | "How well does this match a problem you have?" + "What problem, if any?" |

Plus the **yes-saying** structural fix: behaviour before attitude; scatter agree/disagree items; add a reverse-worded check ("I rarely think about running out of money").

## 7. Survey & interview guide — financial advisers (full)

_Hybrid: works as survey, best as interview. Section A (how paid) first — every later answer reads through it._

### A — Adviser profile & how paid
- A1 Role: Bank RM / Insurer-tied agent / IFA (FA firm) / Fee-only/fee-based planner / Wealth/private banker / Other
- A2 Paid mainly by: Commission / Salary + targets / Salary only / Client fees (AUM% or flat) / Mix
- A3 Years advising: <2 / 2–5 / 6–10 / 11–20 / 20+
- A4 Active clients: <25 / 25–75 / 76–150 / 150+
- A5 Share at/near retirement (55+): <10% / 10–25% / 26–50% / >50%
- A6 You/firm MAS-licensed to advise? Yes / In progress / No / Not sure

### B — Decumulation problem from your chair
- B1 How often do clients ask "how much can I safely spend?" (Almost every / Often / Sometimes / Rarely / Never)
- B2 How well do current tools answer it? (Very well → Not at all)
- B3 (Open) Walk me through what you do today when asked.
- B4 (L) "Decumulation is harder to advise on than accumulation."
- B5 (Open) Where do you get stuck/unsure on retirement income?
- B6 (L) "Many of my retired clients spend less than they safely could."

### C — Tools today
- C1 (MC) What do you use for decumulation planning? (Insurer tools / Bank platform / Own spreadsheets / CPF calculators / 3rd-party software (name) / Nothing structured)
- C2 (Open) What's missing from your current tools?
- C3 (SC) Importance that a tool shows assumptions/workings? (Essential → Not important)

### D — Reaction to Enough *(show adviser-view mock)*

- E5 (L) "If a tool didn't help me sell/place products, I'd have less incentive to introduce it." *(reverse-read for fee-based)*
- E6 (Open) Would recommending a neutral tool sit awkwardly with your firm's products/targets?

- F1 (SC) Who pays? Me / My firm / Bank platform team / The client / Should be free
- F2 (SC) Would you personally pay monthly if it saved time & won trust? Yes / Maybe / No
- F3 (Num) If yes/maybe, reasonable $/adviser/month?
- F4 (SC+open) Prefer it: White-labelled / Co-branded / Independent brand — why?
- F5 (L) "A branded plain-English plan to hand clients would help me win/keep clients."

### G — Adoption & integration barriers
- G1 (MC) Biggest barriers? (Compliance/firm approval / Data entry / Client data-privacy / Learning curve / Workflow fit / Cost / Trust in numbers)
- G2 (SC) How should client data get in? (Auto-connect / I enter / Client enters / Mix)
- G3 (L) "My compliance team would need to approve it first."

## 8. Interview guide — retirees & near-retirees (semi-structured)

**Length 30–40 min · 1-to-1, recorded with permission.**

**Opening (read warmly):** "Thank you. We're students studying how Singaporeans manage money in retirement. No right or wrong answers — I'm interested in *your* experience. It's anonymous. Okay if I record? You can stop or skip anytime."

**Technique:** ask then stay quiet; favour "tell me about a time…/walk me through…"; follow with "why?/tell me more"; **don't pitch** until Part 6.

**Part 1 — Warm-up & their world (5 min)** — "Are you fully retired or still working a bit? Tell me about your typical week." Probes: how long retired/how far off; how you spend time & money.

**Part 2 — How they think about spending (8 min) — core.** "Walk me through how you decide how much to spend in a typical month." Probes: a method or a feel? ever unsure too much/too little? a time you held back on something you wanted — what were you thinking? a time you treated yourself — how did you feel after?
> *Listen for:* spontaneous mention of under-spending out of fear vs genuinely tight. This is the wedge. Capture exact words.

**Part 3 — Worries (5 min)** — "When you think about money for the rest of your life, what worries you most?" Probes: what keeps you up? (if not raised) outliving savings? where do healthcare & rising prices sit?
> *Listen for:* their ranking in their own voice; if healthcare/inflation dominate and "how much can I spend" never surfaces, reframing needed.

**Part 4 — CPF & other money (5 min)** — "How does CPF LIFE fit your retirement picture?" Probes: understand the payout? how think about drawing down other savings? faced top-up/payout choices — how did that go?
> *Listen for:* CPF LIFE as "enough" vs a floor with a confusing gap above.

**Part 5 — Who they trust (5 min)** — "When you need to make a money decision, who/what do you turn to?" Probes: ever used an adviser/bank — how was it? a time you trusted or didn't trust advice — what made the difference? a free bank tool — how would you feel?
> *Listen for:* what drives trust in their words; don't prompt "neutrality"; whether bank involvement raises/lowers trust.

**Part 6 — Reacting to the idea (5–7 min) — show concept HERE.** Show a one-screen mock: "Some students are imagining an app, Enough — one number for roughly how much you can safely spend each month, from your CPF, savings & desired lifestyle, explained in plain words." "What's your honest first reaction?" Probes: speaks to a real problem? which part? trust the number — why/why not? use it yourself or someone helps — who? connect CPF/bank info — how does that feel? what's missing/confusing?
> *Listen for:* polite enthusiasm vs genuine pull; "who'd set it up for me"; "I'd want a person to confirm"; data-sharing hesitation (feasibility gate).

**Part 7 — Close (2 min)** — "If a tool like this worked well, the one thing it would need to do for *you*?" "Anything I should have asked but didn't?" "Open to seeing an early version?" Thank them.

## 9. Interview guide — financial advisers (semi-structured)

**Length 30–45 min · 1-to-1, recorded with permission.**

**Opening:** "Thanks for your time. We research retirement-income planning and adviser tools. Interested in your real experience — what works, what's frustrating. Anonymous, no right answers. Okay to record?"

**Technique:** peer conversation — let them be the expert. Establish how they're paid early & naturally. The crux is Part 4.

**Part 1 — Their practice (5 min)** — "Tell me about your practice — who are your clients, what do they come to you for?" Probes: share at/near retirement? tied/independent/fee-based? what do clients really pay you for?
> *Listen for:* remuneration model — everything splits on this.

**Part 2 — The decumulation problem (8 min)** — "When a client nears retirement and asks 'how much can I actually spend?' — walk me through what you do." Probes: how often? tools/methods? where least sure/messy? harder than accumulation — why? clients under- or over-spend?
> *Listen for:* a real tooling gap; advisers also seeing underspending (supply-side corroboration).

**Part 3 — Tools today (5 min)** — "What are you using now, and what's missing?" Probes: a time a tool let you down / client distrusted a projection; how much does showing the workings matter?

**Part 4 — Reacting + channel question (10–12 min) — crux.** Show adviser-view mock: "Enough produces a client's safe monthly-spend number from CPF, savings & lifestyle, with plain-English explanations. It's neutral — recommends/sells no products. Use with clients or hand to them." "Honest first reaction — where helps, where hinders?" Probes: use in meetings / to prep / hand to clients? what wouldn't you trust? **(key, neutral)** "It earns no product commission and recommends nothing to buy — strength, limitation, or problem?" if a client already used it, how change your conversation? "Would a tool that doesn't help place products be one you'd have reason to introduce? Be honest." would recommending a neutral tool sit awkwardly with firm products/targets?
> *Listen for — headline finding:* does reaction split by pay model? Commission/target advisers may signal low incentive; fee-based may love it. Capture exact language. If the neutrality that's your moat is what cools advisers, the channel needs rethinking; fee-based planners become the beachhead.

**Part 5 — Money & adoption (6 min)** — "If this were good, how would it realistically get paid for and adopted in your world?" Probes: who pays — you/firm/platform/client? white-label vs co-brand vs independent — why? what would compliance say? biggest practical barrier?

**Part 6 — Regulatory read (4 min)** — "Does a 'safe spending number' cross into regulated advice?" Probes: where's the educational-vs-personalised-advice line? licensing/compliance pitfalls?

**Part 7 — Close (2 min)** — "One thing to build first to make it useful to *you*?" "Anything I should've asked?" "Open to a follow-up or piloting with a client?" Thank them.

## 10. Other respondent groups to interview

Priority order, with why each can change the business model:
1. **Adult children of retirees (40s–50s)** — may be the real *operator* and even *buyer* (sandwich generation). Could flip GTM to adult-child-as-buyer. *Highest priority alongside retirees.*

3. **Bank / insurer / employer-wellness decision-makers** — the actual B2B2C *customers*: white-label appetite, price, procurement/compliance bar, and whether neutrality is a feature or a problem for their product-sales model. *This is where revenue comes from.*
4. **Regulatory / compliance expert (ex-MAS, fintech lawyer, robo-adviser operator)** — pressure-test the phased MAS path; whether "safe-to-spend number" is regulated advice sooner than assumed.
5. **CPF / decumulation domain experts (academics, CPF policy, actuaries)** — validate the engine's SG calibration (longevity, healthcare inflation, CPF LIFE) and lend quantitative credibility.
6. *(Optional)* **Lapsed users of existing retirement tools** — why retirement tools don't stick; cheapest way to avoid a known failure.

## 11. Cross-cutting recommendations

- **Interviews first, survey later.** Run 8–15 interviews each (retirees + adult children + advisers) to discover the real problem and fix leading/confusing questions *before* spending your finite survey sample.
- **Recruit for range, not volume.** Retirees: mix asset levels & ages. Advisers: deliberately include each pay model (bank RM, tied agent, IFA, fee-based) — the point is to see whether the reaction *splits*.
- **Three kill-criteria to watch across all research:**
  1. **Is the underspending/"permission-to-spend" wedge real?** (retiree Part 2/3; adviser B6)
  2. **Does the bank channel help adoption or clash with the neutrality moat?** (retiree F3/F4 + I3; adviser Section E)
  3. **Will this cohort use an app and share financial data — or is it really a family tool?** (retiree J2/J4)
- **Watch say-vs-do.** When someone says "I'd use it," follow with "tell me about the last time you tried something like that" to test against real behaviour.
- **Same interviewer; full notes within an hour** while memory is fresh.
