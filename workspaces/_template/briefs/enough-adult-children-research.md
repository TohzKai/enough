# Enough — Adult-Children Survey + Business-Model Implications

_Addendum to enough-research-pack.md (§10 named adult children as the #1 priority group).
Compiled 2026-06-30. SC=single choice, MC=multiple choice, L=Likert 1–5, Open=free text,
Num=numeric. Neutral-worded per the pack's leading-question discipline._

The research pack treats the **retiree** as the user. This instrument tests a different
hypothesis: the **adult child (40s–50s, "sandwich generation") is the real operator and
possibly the buyer.** If true, it changes who we sell to, how we price, and what we build.

> **Scope note (updated 2026-07-01 — un-parked, with a deliberate limit).** Adult children are
> adopted as **a target customer for the B2C channel** — specifically the buyer of the direct
> **family tier** (see enough-session-record §2.10 / the B2B2C work). They are **NOT** declared
> Enough's **overall primary paying customer**: the primary revenue engine remains **non-bank
> B2B2C** (employer-wellness → fee-only IFAs → insurers; partners pay), with the family tier
> sitting *on top*. Whether adult children ever become the *overall* primary buyer is **deferred
> pending fieldwork** — chiefly E3 (would a child pay as a gift?) and G1 (which way the
> inheritance motive cuts?). Read Part C's "primary buyer" language through this limit: adult
> child = the B2C/family-tier buyer + co-operator, retiree = the beneficiary, B2B2C partners =
> the lead revenue source.

---

## Bias traps specific to adult children (fix before fielding)

1. **Don't assume they want to control the parent's money.** Many won't, and asking as if
   they do produces false positives. Offer "it's entirely their money / not my place" as a
   real option everywhere.
2. **Don't assume the inheritance motive — in either direction.** Some want parents to spend
   and enjoy; some quietly hope assets are preserved; many are conflicted. Ask neutrally with
   balanced options; never imply a "right" answer.
3. **Acquiescence + social desirability.** "Helping aging parents" is socially virtuous, so
   people over-claim involvement and generosity. Ask *behaviour* ("what have you actually
   done") before *attitude* ("how much would you help").
4. **Don't sell.** No "wouldn't it be reassuring to…" framing — the pack's rule.

## Part A — Strategy-annotated survey (adult children)

### Section A — Screening & situation
*Hypothesis: we can reach involved adult children. Risk: surveying people with no living/SG
parent, or no involvement, and over-reading.*
- A1 (SC) Do you have a parent (or parent-in-law) aged 60+ living in Singapore? *(Yes / No→screen out)*
- A2 (SC) Your age: <35 / 35–44 / 45–54 / 55–64 / 65+
- A3 (SC) Are you currently supporting children **and** parents at the same time? *(Yes both / Children only / Parents only / Neither)*
- A4 (SC) How involved are you in your parents' money matters? *(Very — I manage much of it / Somewhat — I help with decisions / A little — they ask occasionally / Not at all)*
- A5 (SC) Do you contribute money to your parents' living costs? *(Regularly / Occasionally / No / They support themselves fully)*
> A4 is the real screen: the "operator" hypothesis only lives among the involved. Hold the
> "not at all" group separately — their reasons are their own finding.

### Section B — Current involvement (behaviour before attitude)
*Hypothesis: adult children already act as informal operators/advisers. Risk: if they don't,
the family-operator model is aspirational, not real.*
- B1 (MC) Which have you actually done for your parents in the last year? *(Helped set up/use a banking or gov app · Helped a CPF decision e.g. payout/top-up · Helped decide a big purchase or how much to spend · Reviewed their insurance · Managed/queried their investments · Set up Singpass/Myinfo · None)*
- B2 (SC) Who in the family is most "hands-on" with your parents' finances? *(Me / A sibling / My parent themselves / A spouse / An adviser / No one really)*
- B3 (SC) When your parents make a money decision, how often are you involved? *(Almost always / Often / Sometimes / Rarely / Never)*
- B4 (Open) Describe the last time you helped a parent with a money matter — what was it?

### Section C — Worries about parents' finances (the problem from their chair)
*Hypothesis: adult children carry real, felt anxiety about parents' money — possibly more than
the parents do. Risk: if they're not worried, there's no buyer pull.*
- C1 (L) "I worry my parents could run low on money later in life."
- C2 (L) "I'm not sure my parents know how much they can safely spend."
- C3 (L) "If my parents needed money later, I would likely have to help."
- C4 (SC) Which worries you most about your parents' later years? *(They outlive their savings / A big health or long-term-care bill / They deny themselves and live too frugally / Becoming a financial burden on me/siblings / I genuinely don't worry / Not sure)*
- C5 (SC) Compared with your parents, you think they are: *(More worried about money than they need to be / About right / Not worried enough / I can't tell)*
- C6 (Open) What is your single biggest concern about your parents' finances?
> C4 is doublesided on purpose — "too frugal" (our wedge) competes fairly with "run out"
> (the opposite fear). The split tells us which story sells to the child.

### Section D — The operator hypothesis (would they run the tool)
*Hypothesis: the digitally-fluent child sets up and operates a tool on the parent's behalf —
solving the retiree data-entry / digital-readiness feasibility gate. Risk: parents may not
accept the involvement; children may not want the responsibility.*
- D1 (SC) If a tool could show your parents a safe monthly spending amount, who would realistically set it up? *(Me, for them / Them, with my help / Them, alone / No one)*
- D2 (SC) Would you be willing to enter or connect your parents' financial information into such a tool? *(Yes, if they agreed / Only if they did it with me / No, too sensitive / No, they'd refuse)*
- D3 (L) "My parents would be comfortable with me helping run something like this."
- D4 (SC) Would your parents accept a spending figure more readily if **you** were involved in it? *(Yes, more / No difference / No, less / Not sure)*
- D5 (Open) What would make helping your parents with a tool like this easier — or harder?
> D2+D3 are the feasibility crux: the operator model needs both the child's willingness AND
> the parent's acceptance. Either missing breaks it.

### Section E — The buyer hypothesis & willingness to pay
*Hypothesis: adult children have higher WTP than frugal retirees and would pay for a parent.
Risk: the whole "who pays" reframe rests here.*
- E1 (SC) Would you pay for a tool that helped your parents plan their retirement spending? *(Yes, a monthly subscription / Yes, a one-time cost / Only if free / Only if my employer or their bank provided it)*
- E2 (Van Westendorp, 4 Num) For such a tool, at what monthly price is it: too cheap to trust / good value / starting to feel expensive / too expensive?
- E3 (SC) Would you pay for it as a **gift / on their behalf**, even if they wouldn't pay themselves? *(Yes / Maybe / No)*
- E4 (SC) If you could manage **both** parents (or several relatives) in one place, would that be worth more to you? *(Yes clearly / A little / No)*
- E5 (SC) Who *should* pay for something like this? *(Me / My parents / Government / Their bank / My employer as a benefit)*
> E3 is the headline WTP question: a "gift" framing among children is the most plausible
> escape from the retiree frugality paradox. E4 tests a multi-relative pricing tier.

### Section F — Trust, neutrality & the bank channel
*Hypothesis: children, like retirees, prefer a neutral source for a parent's money. Risk: they
may instead trust a big institution more for safety.*
- F1 (SC) Who would you trust most to tell your parents how much they can safely spend? *(An independent app / Their bank / A human adviser / CPF Board / A government agency / Me/the family / No one)*
- F2 (MC) What would make you trust such a tool for your parents? **(pick up to 2)** *(Shows its assumptions / Plain-English explanations / Backed by real market data / Endorsed by CPF or MAS / No commission / Recommended by an adviser / A person we can call)*
- F3 (SC) If the tool were provided by your parents' bank or insurer, your trust would be: *(More / No difference / Less / Depends)*
- F4 (Open) What would worry you most about using an app for your parents' retirement money?

### Section G — Family dynamics & the "permission to spend" tension
*Hypothesis: children genuinely want parents to enjoy their money (supports the wedge). Risk:
inheritance motive or "controlling" dynamics quietly cut the other way — must surface honestly.*
- G1 (SC) When you think about your parents' savings, which is closest to your view? *(I'd rather they spend it and enjoy life / I'd hope some is preserved for the family / A mix of both / It's entirely their money — not my concern)*
- G2 (L) "I would feel comfortable encouraging my parents to spend more if I knew it was safe."
- G3 (L) "Talking to my parents about their money is difficult or sensitive." *(reverse-context check)*
- G4 (SC) Would a tool that lets the family see a parent's plan feel: *(Helpful — keeps us aligned / Useful but sensitive / Intrusive — parents wouldn't want it / Depends who can see what)*
- G5 (Open) How do money conversations usually go in your family?
> G1 is the conflict-of-interest probe, worded so no answer is "wrong." If "preserve for the
> family" dominates, the child-as-buyer introduces a misalignment the product must manage
> (Enough must stay on the *parent's* side). G4 sizes the appetite for shared access.

### Section H — Concept reaction *(show description + one mock screen)*
> "Enough gives a retiree one number — how much they can safely spend each month — from their
> CPF, savings and desired lifestyle, explained in plain words, with an optional shared view
> for family. It sells no products."
- H1 (L) "I understand what this does."
- H2 (L+Open) "This speaks to something my family deals with." (1–5) + What, if anything, does it solve for you?
- H3 (SC) Who would this be most for, in your family? *(My parents directly / Me, to help them / Both together / No one)*
- H4 (L) "I would try this for my family."
- H5 (Open) What's confusing or missing?

### Section I — Feature prioritisation (family lens)
*Hypothesis: the shared/family features rank higher for children than they did for retirees.*
- I1 (rank/MaxDiff, pick top 3): Safe monthly-spend number · "Will it last to 95?" reading · "Big health bill" test · Today's-vs-future-dollars view · CPF top-up & payout guidance · Trips/gifts/inheritance planning · Plain-English "why" · **Shared family view / multi-parent dashboard** · Alerts to me if something needs attention.
> If "shared view" + "alerts to me" rank top-3 here but didn't for retirees, that's direct
> evidence to pull family features forward in the roadmap.

### Section J — Digital & data
- J1 (SC) Comfort using and setting up phone apps (yours and others')? *(Very → Not at all)*
- J2 (L) "I would worry about my parents' financial data being leaked or misused."
- J3 (SC) Who would hold the login / control access for a parent's plan? *(The parent / Me / Shared / Not sure)*

### Section K — Close
- K1 (SC) Open to a 20-min follow-up interview? *(Yes + contact / No)*
- K2 (Open) Anything else a tool like this should do for families like yours?

> **Non-negotiable core (kill-criteria for the adult-child thesis):** D2+D3 (operator
> feasibility), E1+E3 (buyer/WTP & gift), C1/C2/C4 (is the worry real and which way it points),
> G1 (inheritance conflict). Trimmable: B4, F4, G5, I1.

## Part C — If adult children are a target consumer, how the model changes

Treating adult children as a target reframes Enough from a **single-user retiree app** into a
**family product with a payer–user split**. Recommendation up front: make the adult child the
primary **buyer and co-operator**, and keep the retiree the primary **beneficiary and decision-
maker**. That single move solves three of Enough's hardest problems at once — and introduces
one new tension to manage. Below, what changes, why, and the trade-off.

### 1. Who pays changes — and it fixes the frugality paradox
- **What changes:** the buyer is no longer the frugal retiree (low willingness-to-pay, §4.1 of
  the risk doc) but a mid-career adult child — digitally fluent, income-earning, and *anxious
  about their parents*, which is exactly the emotion that converts to payment.
- **Implication:** introduce **gift / "pay on their behalf" subscriptions** and a **multi-parent
  family plan** (one buyer, several relatives). This is the most credible answer to "retirees
  won't pay."
- **Trade-off (honest):** the person paying isn't the person using it daily, so the value has to
  be visible to *both* — the child needs reassurance, the parent needs the spending number.
  Two audiences to satisfy in one product.

### 2. Distribution opens a second, stronger channel
- **What changes:** you can now reach the buyer where they actually are. **Employer-wellness
  B2B2C** sharpens from "retiree benefit" to **"help your aging parents" as a benefit for
  mid-career staff** — the sandwich generation is a large, reachable, employed segment.
- **Implication:** add employer/HR and family-oriented channels alongside the bank/insurer ones;
  the pitch to employers ("support your stressed sandwich-generation employees") is more
  distinctive than a generic retirement tool.
- **Trade-off:** more channels to build and support; don't over-extend before one proves out.

### 3. It solves the feasibility gate (data entry + digital readiness)
- **What changes:** the retiree research's existential risk — "most won't connect data and the
  elderly struggle with apps" (§J2/J4) — is **directly mitigated** when the digitally-fluent
  child is the operator who sets it up and enters/connects the data.
- **Implication:** design onboarding for the **child operating on the parent's behalf**, with a
  clean **consent flow** where the parent authorises. The "shared family view" moves from a
  Wave-3 "delight" feature to a **Wave-1 core capability** — roles and permissions (parent =
  owner/decision-maker; child = operator/viewer; sibling = viewer) become foundational, not
  optional.
- **Trade-off:** multi-user access, permissions, and audit are real engineering scope added
  early; the simple single-user MVP gets more complex.

### 4. The product needs two faces
- **What changes:** one simple, calm "your monthly paycheck" view for the parent; a richer
  analytical view plus **alerts** ("Dad's plan needs a look") for the child.
- **Implication:** invest in role-based UX and notifications. The child wants *oversight without
  intrusion*; the parent wants *confidence without surveillance*.

### 5. A new conflict of interest — and how neutrality answers it
- **What changes:** a child who pays/operates may have an interest *opposed* to the product's
  core purpose. Enough exists to give parents **permission to spend** (draw down the estate); a
  child with an inheritance motive wants the opposite. (Survey G1 measures which way this cuts.)
- **Implication / the strategic clarity:** Enough must be explicitly **parent-centric — "on the
  parent's side."** This actually *extends the neutrality wedge*: Enough is neutral not only
  versus product-sellers (banks, advisers) but versus the family's own competing incentives.
  Concretely: the safe-spend number always optimises the *parent's* wellbeing; legacy/bequest is
  an explicit, parent-set input (the Freedom & Legacy feature), never a silent default; the
  parent — not the child — sees and confirms the number.
- **Trade-off:** you may occasionally tell a paying child something they don't want to hear
  ("your parents can afford to spend more"). That's the right call — and it's the same
  integrity that makes the neutrality moat believable.

### 6. Regulatory & privacy gains a wrinkle
- **What changes:** when the child operates/pays but the finances and any advice concern the
  *parent*, the **client is the parent.** Consent (PDPA) and suitability must be the parent's,
  even when the child does the typing.
- **Implication:** build explicit parent-authorisation into onboarding; document that data
  access is parent-consented; any future personalised advice (post-licence) is addressed to the
  parent. This is manageable but must be designed in, not bolted on.

### 7. TAM and retention improve
- **What changes:** the addressable *buyer* pool widens from "retirees who will pay" to "the much
  larger pool of mid-career children, each potentially managing 1–2 parents." Family accounts
  with multiple stakeholders also tend to **retain longer** (someone keeps it active for decades).
- **Trade-off:** none material — this is largely upside, provided the parent-centric integrity
  (point 5) holds.

### Recommendation (the pick) — as scoped by the 2026-07-01 un-park decision
**Adopt adult children as the target customer for the B2C channel (the direct family-tier buyer)
and co-operator, with the retiree as the beneficiary — a payer–user split, not a target swap —
WITHOUT yet declaring them Enough's overall primary paying customer.** The lead revenue engine
stays **non-bank B2B2C** (employer-wellness → IFAs → insurers); the adult-child-paid family tier
sits on top. This still captures the high-leverage wins (it fixes consumer willingness-to-pay in
the one segment that has it, eases the data-entry/digital-readiness gate, feeds the
employer-wellness channel, and widens the buyer pool) — but it does **not** bet the whole
go-to-market on the adult child until fieldwork settles E3 (gift-WTP) and G1 (inheritance
direction).

The cost is real and bounded: shared-access/roles/consent must come forward into Wave 1 (more
early scope), and the product must stay rigorously **parent-centric** to neutralise the
inheritance conflict. Validate before committing with the four kill-criteria above — especially
**E3 (would a child pay as a gift)** and **G1 (which way the inheritance motive cuts)**. If the
gift-WTP is strong and most children genuinely want parents to enjoy their money, this becomes
the spine of the go-to-market. If "preserve the inheritance" dominates, keep the retiree as the
buyer and treat the child as operator-only.

What I would *not* do: drop the retiree as the user. Enough's credibility depends on serving the
person whose money it is; the child is how you reach and fund that, not who you serve.
