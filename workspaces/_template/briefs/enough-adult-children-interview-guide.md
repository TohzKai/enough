# Enough — Interview Guide: Adult Children of Retirees (semi-structured)

_Completes the #1-priority instrument the research pack (§10) flagged as pending. Companion to
`enough-adult-children-research.md` (the survey + business-model implications) and
`enough-research-pack.md` (§8 retirees, §9 advisers). Compiled 2026-07-01._

**Run this BEFORE the adult-children survey.** Per the pack's method note, interviews come first —
they discover the real problem in the family's own words and catch leading/confusing wording
before the finite survey sample is spent. This guide de-risks the two questions that gate the
deferred "should adult children be Enough's overall primary buyer?" decision: **E3 (would a child
pay, incl. as a gift?)** and **G1 (which way the inheritance motive cuts?)**.

**Length 30–40 min · 1-to-1, recorded with permission.**
**Recruit:** adult children (≈40s–50s) with a parent or parent-in-law 60+ in Singapore; bias
toward those with SOME involvement in a parent's finances, but include 2–3 hands-off ones (their
reasons are their own finding).

---

## Opening (read warmly)
"Thank you for helping. We're students studying how Singaporean families manage money as parents
grow older. There are no right or wrong answers — I'm interested in *your* experience. It's
anonymous, and you can skip anything or stop anytime. Is it okay if I record, just so I don't
have to scribble?"

## Technique (for the interviewer)
- A **peer conversation**, not a survey read-out. Ask, then **stay quiet** — let them fill the
  silence.
- Favour **"tell me about a time…" / "walk me through…"**; follow with "why?" / "tell me more."
- **Behaviour before attitude** — ask what they've actually *done* before what they *think*.
- **Do NOT pitch Enough until Part 6.** Everything before it must be about their real life.
- The inheritance topic (Part 4) is **sensitive** — stay neutral and non-judgmental; make clear
  every view is normal.

---

## Part 1 — Warm-up & their world (5 min)
"Tell me a bit about your family — your parents, and who else you're looking after."
Probes: How far into retirement are your parents? How's their health? Do they live with you /
near you / independently? Are you also raising children (i.e. supporting both generations)?
> *Listen for:* the "sandwich" load; how close/involved they are day-to-day.

## Part 2 — Current involvement in parents' money (7 min) — behaviour first
"Walk me through the last time you helped a parent with something money-related."
Probes: Who in the family is most hands-on with your parents' finances? Do you help with things
like CPF decisions, insurance, their banking app, or big purchases? Do you contribute to their
living costs? How did setting any of that up go?
> *Listen for:* are they ALREADY an informal operator, or hands-off? (Tests the operator premise
> from the behaviour side, before any tool is mentioned.)

## Part 3 — Worries about parents' finances (6 min) — the problem from their chair
"When you think about your parents' money lasting the rest of their lives, what, if anything,
worries you?"
Probes (only if not raised): Running out / outliving savings? A big health or long-term-care bill?
That they're *too careful* and not enjoying it? Becoming a financial burden on you or siblings?
> *Listen for — DOUBLE-SIDED, this is the wedge test:* which worry dominates in their own words —
> **"they'll run out"** (fear) vs **"they're too frugal to enjoy it"** (our permission-to-spend
> wedge)? Capture their exact phrasing. If neither surfaces and it's all health/inflation, the
> wedge is weaker than assumed.

## Part 4 — Family money dynamics & the inheritance tension (6 min) — sensitive, neutral
"How do money conversations usually go in your family?"
Probes: Is it easy or awkward to talk to your parents about their money? When you think about their
savings, what's your honest view — that you'd rather they spend and enjoy it, that you'd hope some
is kept for the family, or a mix? Would you feel comfortable *encouraging* them to spend more if
you knew it was safe?
> *Listen for — G1 (the inheritance direction):* which way the motive genuinely cuts — "spend &
> enjoy" vs "preserve some" vs conflicted — and whether the topic is taboo. Do NOT lead or judge;
> let the balanced options compete. This is the single most important thing to learn here, because
> a child-buyer whose instinct is "preserve the inheritance" is misaligned with the product's
> "spend it down safely" purpose.

## Part 5 — The operator question (5 min)
"Imagine there were a simple tool that showed your parents how much they could safely spend each
month. Realistically, who in your family would set it up and keep it going?"
Probes: Would you set it up / enter or connect their information? Would you be comfortable doing
that with their agreement? Would your *parents* be comfortable with you helping run it? Would they
trust a spending figure more, or less, if you were involved?
> *Listen for:* BOTH halves of operator feasibility — the child's willingness AND the parent's
> acceptance. Either one missing breaks the operator model.

## Part 6 — Reacting to the idea, and paying (7 min) — SHOW THE CONCEPT HERE
Show a one-screen mock + a plain description: *"Some students are imagining an app, Enough — it
gives a retiree one number: roughly how much they can safely spend each month, from their CPF,
savings and the lifestyle they want, explained in plain words — with an optional shared view for
family. It sells no products."*
"What's your honest first reaction?"
Probes: Does this speak to something your family actually deals with? Who is it most for — your
parents, you, or both? Would you use it?
**Then the buyer / WTP question (the reason this interview exists):**
"Would you pay for something like this for your parents?" → "Would you pay for it **as a gift, on
their behalf**, even if they wouldn't pay for it themselves?" → "What would it need to do to be
worth paying for?"
> *Listen for — E3 (gift-WTP):* a genuine "yes, I'd pay for my parents" vs a polite concept-nod.
> Who do they instinctively think *should* pay — them, their parents, an employer, the bank?
> This is the evidence that decides whether the family tier has real willingness-to-pay.

## Part 7 — Trust, neutrality & data (4 min)
"How would you feel about your parents' whole financial picture — accounts across different banks —
sitting inside one app?"
Probes: How comfortable would you be connecting that data? If the app were provided by your
parents' *bank*, would you trust it more, less, or no different — why? What would worry you most?
> *Listen for:* the neutrality preference (does a bank-provided version raise or lower trust?) and
> data-sharing hesitancy — both feed the channel + data-firewall decisions.

## Part 8 — Close (2 min)
"If a tool like this worked really well, what's the one thing it would need to do for *your*
family?" · "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?" · "Would you be open to seeing an
early version?" Thank them.

---

## Kill-criteria mapping (what each part is really testing)
| Deferred question / kill-criterion | Where it's tested |
|---|---|
| Operator feasibility (child willing AND parent accepts) | Part 2 (behaviour) + Part 5 (stated) |
| The wedge — is "too frugal / permission to spend" real, or is it all "run out"? | Part 3 (double-sided, unprompted) |
| **G1 — which way the inheritance motive cuts** (gates "overall primary buyer") | Part 4 |
| **E3 — gift-WTP** (gates the family tier's willingness-to-pay) | Part 6 |
| Data-sharing feasibility + neutrality preference | Part 7 |

## Method reminders (from the pack's cross-cutting recommendations)
- Run **8–15** of these before fielding the adult-children survey; recruit for RANGE (involved and
  hands-off; parents at different wealth levels).
- **Watch say-vs-do:** when someone says "I'd pay / I'd use it," follow with "tell me about the
  last time you actually paid for or set up something like this for your parents."
- Same interviewer where possible; write up full notes within the hour while memory is fresh.
